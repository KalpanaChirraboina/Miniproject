Chandra Kanta Murasingh Profile Details

Chandra Kanta Murasingh was a distinguished poet from Tripura, celebrated for his poignant works in Kokborok and Bengali. Born in Tuiwandal, he captured the socio-cultural struggles and natural beauty of Tripura in his poetry, reflecting the region’s indigenous ethos. A recipient of the Sahitya Akademi’s Bhasha Samman, his translations and original works enriched Northeast Indian literature until his passing in 2023.

Books and Publications

    Haping Garingo Chibuksa Ringo (1987): A collection of Kokborok poems.

    Holong Kok Sao Bolong Bisingo (1995): Poems reflecting Tripura’s indigenous life.

    Lok Chethuwang Lok (1999): Poetic narratives of Tripura’s landscape.

    Pindi Watawi Pin (1999): Kokborok poetry on social issues.

    Ruphaini Buduk Ani Nogo (2002): A blend of nature and social commentary.

Early Life and Education

Born on April 1, 1957, in Tuiwandal, Tripura, Chandra Kanta Murasingh grew up in a family of shifting cultivators. His father, Syampada Murasingh, ensured he attended Ramesh School in Udaipur, despite early hardships. His exposure to Bengali literature during his time in Imphal (1973–1976) while working for the Indian Postal Service shaped his poetic voice, blending indigenous Kokborok traditions with literary influences.

Political Career

While not a politician, Murasingh’s poetry addressed political turmoil in Tripura, critiquing violence between extremists and the Indian Army. His works chronicled the marginalized voices of indigenous communities, advocating for their socio-cultural identity. As a member of the Tripura Tribal Language Commission, he promoted Kokborok, amplifying its literary presence.

Literary Contributions

Murasingh’s poetry in Kokborok and Bengali captured Tripura’s socio-political struggles and natural beauty. His translation of Rabindranath Tagore’s Gitanjali into Kokborok was a landmark achievement. With seven poetry collections, his works appeared in Indian Literature, Oxford, and Penguin anthologies, elevating Kokborok’s global reach. He also worked on musical notation for Kokborok folk songs.

Selected Poems

  • The Thud of Boots (1999): Reflects on violence in Tripura.
  • Madhavi Fragrance (2009): Blends nature with the acrid reality of conflict.
  • Rivulet’s Cadence (1995): Evokes Tripura’s lost natural harmony.
  • Seven Hills (1987): Celebrates Tripura’s landscape.
  • Voices of the Marginalized (2002): Chronicles indigenous struggles.

Major Achievements

  • Received Sahitya Akademi’s Bhasha Samman Award (1997) for Kokborok literature.
  • Awarded “True Legend of North-East India” by The Telegraph Group.
  • Translated Tagore’s Gitanjali into Kokborok, a monumental work.
  • Received Rabindra Puraskar from Tripura State Government.
  • Participated in Bangladesh’s National Poetry Festival thrice.

Legacy and Honors

Murasingh’s work immortalized Tripura’s indigenous culture, earning him tributes at Nazrul Kala Kshetra and Rabindra Satabarshiki Bhawan after his passing in 2023. As Director of the Northeast Colloquial Language Centre, he promoted Kokborok. His poetry remains a beacon for Northeast Indian literature, with posthumous honors from Tripura’s Chief Minister and literary bodies.

Famous Quotes

“The fragrance of flowery garlands haunts the seven hills.”
“The rivulet’s song is drowned by the thud of boots.”
“Poetry is the voice of a people’s unspoken pain.”

Personal Life and Children

Chandra Kanta Murasingh was married and had two sons and a daughter. Living in Abhoynagar, Agartala, he balanced his career at Tripura Gramin Bank with his literary pursuits. Known for his amiable nature, he remained deeply connected to his ancestral village of Tuiwandal, where his last rites were performed in 2023.
